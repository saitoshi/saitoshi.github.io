### We learn by asking questions. But the way we ask our question changes the outcomes. ###

> “Always ask why and that is how you learn.”

 Most young children may have the tendency to ask other adults why. The reason being is that young children lacked the vocabulary skills necessary to ask concise and clear questions, therefore, as a result they ask “why", “what”, and “how”. However, as children grow up they gain the vocabulary skills to ask more concise and clear questions. But, just because they have more vocabulary skills, does that guarantee intelligent questions?

However, before asking questions, regardless if it is smart or not, the first thing one must do is to check whether the question is being asked in the appropriate environment. For example, you cannot ask an English Major a question regarding Vector Analysis. You might get a response, but not an answer. Furthermore, the accurate environment usually has people with high credibility of that certain subject. Therefore, asking a vector question to a person who specializes in the topic seems more effective than asking an English Professor. After checking if the environment is appropriate, the next step is to use the appropriate words to ask that question in a smart way. 
For instance, here are some of the following questions that fall into the not so smart questions.

### <a href = "https://stackoverflow.com/questions/59961664/child-row-above-parent-row-on-datatableQuestion"> Question </a> Child row above parent row on datatable ###

To me, this is an unclear question because I do not know why the user is asking about it. Even if I know the person looking at it understood the terminology used here, there is no precision. A responder has to think and comprehend the question and it might not be what the user wants, but since it lacks precision, who is to blame? It is not the responder, but the person who originally lacked precision.  

So what is an example of a smart question? Typically, a question that typically includes the right volume of information. For example, we might have a lot to ask because we are curious. However, there is a max capacity in which a person can comprehend to respond with an accurate answer. Additionally, one must explain their individual goal and not the steps. Here is a question that I find that is a smart one. 

### <a href = "https://stackoverflow.com/questions/11227809/why-is-processing-a-sorted-array-faster-than-processing-an-unsorted-array"> Question </a> Why is processing a sorted array faster than processing an unsorted array? ###


Compared to the previous question, it begins with the word “Why”. Just with this simple word, the person who attempts to respond can guess the type of the question. Furthermore, it is clear and concise, so any responder can answer without any doubts. However, the response all depends on the type of person who is writing out the answer. It happened to be the person was nice and detailed. Maybe, since the person asked a smart question originally, it attracted a kind person who was able to do a great response. 

Asking is always fine, but we always have to check whether the question is smart and appropriate.
